A Hint of Paradise

A tiny guide to incense sticks—their history, forms, and variations.

What are incense sticks?

Incense is aromatic material that releases fragrant smoke when burned. Stick incense may be cored (a bamboo rod coated in fragrant paste) or coreless (formed entirely from aromatic material). It is used in homes and temples for ceremony, meditation, and atmosphere.

A very short history

The word incense comes from Latin incendere—“to burn.” Incense traveled widely via trade routes linking Egypt, India, China, and beyond. Over centuries, local ingredients and techniques produced distinct traditions—Japanese kōdō (the “way of incense”), Indian agarbatti stick-making, Tibetan and Himalayan rope incense, and Chinese coils and joss sticks.

Popular variations

  • Japanese incense — see also kōdō.
  • Indian incense (Agarbatti)
  • Tibetan incense
  • Bhutanese incense
  • Nepalese incense
  • Chinese incense

Forms & ingredients (at a glance)

Common forms, ingredients, and notes by tradition
Type / Region Typical form Common ingredients Notes
Japanese Coreless sticks; kneaded pellets Sandalwood, aloeswood (agarwood), clove, cinnamon The appreciation art is called kōdō.
Indian (Agarbatti) Bamboo‑cored sticks (masalā or dipped) Wood powders, resins, floral oils India is a leading producer and exporter.
Tibetan / Nepalese / Bhutanese Traditional coreless sticks; rope incense; stickless extruded Herbal Himalayan blends Often earthy; used in monastic settings.
Chinese Bamboo‑cored sticks; coils Sandalwood, agarwood, herbs & spices Common in temples and ancestor rites.
